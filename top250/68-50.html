2011 Vintage, purchased in May of this year, brought back to Sydney Australia and shared with @LaitueGonflable and @tobeerornottobe.

Pours a lovely deep brown, just flecked with hints of ruby. The body is a thick, glutinous blob of chewy malt, and the bubbly inconsistent head is really the subsistent, secondary part of the appearance. Nice fine carbonation. Looks very decent.

Nose is just lovely, and a brilliant archetype of the Firestone Walker barrel-aging genre. Lovely coconut-vanilla characters coming through above a gorgeously thick and sweet malt basis. Slight hints of earthy vegetation coming through, giving a touch of tea and a minerally hint of seaweed. Very nicely accentuated. Lovely.

Taste is smooth, luscious and gorgeous, with big sweetness beautifully worked off the oak tannins and faint acidity. Coconut comes through, along with hints of reduced bourbon, barley sugar and a slick resiny aftertaste. Really smooth, and gorgeously integrated—the characters all meld together really beautifully, and although the complexities are manifold, they form one coherence, blissful whole.

Feel is spectacularly smooth, while remaining quite fluid. The feel is pretty much the only way to truly detect the alcohol as well, sitting as it does as a warmth on the back of the throat.

A gorgeous beer from FW. I love their anniversary barrel blends, but this one shows that they can do something amazing and comparable with a single brew as well. Great stuff.

It may or may not have been the time spent in a barrel, but Firestone Abacus looks like wood. It is such a deep, opaque brown that I can feel splinters just looking at it. Not only is the colour dark, but the complexion is hazy as stale tea. Any initial carbonation obviously became part of the 'angel's share' as this can hardly muster a halo.

This barleywine was a resident of Heaven Hill bourbon barrels for 3 years and is not shy at all about its Alma mater. It is rich, temperately boozy, and showcases a strong barrel character. The bourbon, however, is not overtaxed as the backdrop of charred, honeyed wood is enlivened by notes of dark sticky-sweet fruits.

If the beer has any shortcomings, flavour is certainly not one of them: butterscotch and plum, raisin and chocolate pudding, tree resin and charred wood, custard and astringently steeped tea, the list is endless as forever. It entertains the palate with bourbon-inspired tastes and lots of gooey rich malt. Warming is most definitely a good thing.

The mouthfeel has an almost syrupy viscosity with thick molasses and dark caramel notes. Sweetness, however, is hardly noted due to the char. The fact that you can still sense some of the leafy, earthy East Kent Golding bitterness is nothing short of remarkable. Mind you, it tastes as though it's been dipped in tobacco juice and coconut oil.

At 13% alcohol, Abacus may make walking a delicately poised talent but drinking it - all 1 pint, 6 fluid ounces of it - is far from a challenging task. It may take some time, but that's less about necessity as it is not wanting to ever find the bottom. It offers a complexity that only barrel-aging can offer and, at only a year old, should only get better.

Abacus is a beer for bourbon-barrel lovers. In a way, it exceeds any descriptions I can give of it. In another, more accurate way, it is a sublime blend of rich barleywine and proud Kentucky Bourbon. There's more flavor here in the aftertaste than most beers have in their entirety. This definitely rivals the best American barrel-aged brews. Thanks Sam!

M: Thick and smooth, the bourbon even lending some cinnamon heat to the mix. Thanks, Dan, for this bottle; the howling winds outside give it a seasonal appropriateness.

Sticky and medium-full in body, with moderate, smooth carbonation. Nicely creamy, with lingering booze and sweetness in the finish. A tremendous drinking experience overall.
