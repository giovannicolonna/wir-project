TooGood, Nov 05, 2014
4.68/5  rDev +4.2%
look: 4.5 | smell: 4.5 | taste: 4.75 | feel: 4.75 | overall: 4.75

2014 vintage and my 500th review. Fitting that it should be a variant of the first beer I ever hunted.

Appearance: Pitch black with a softly ebbing dark tan head. Good lacing.

Aroma: Dark malt with creamy waves of vanilla and alcohol. Quite sweet as the barrel aging comes through strong, providing liqueur-esque stone fruit tones. Opens up more as time goes on.

Taste: Starts with a moderate resinous bitterness that supports a huge dosing of dark malt flavor that is incredibly smooth. Sweet cream, vanilla, and toffee intertwine to provide a hell of a depth of flavor. The bitterness hits a high point in the finish along with the first tinge of alcohol. The rye whiskey barrels come through the strongest immediately after the finish with a spicy rye character that is as harsh as it is delicious. The aftertaste is almost exactly the same as what one would find after a sip of bourbon; alcohol burn like a flame licking your whole palate before quickly smoothing into layers of vanilla and sweet toffee. The choice of second use barrels is really well done here. The harsh whiskey aftertaste plays so well to balance the otherwise sticky sweet flavor. Really well done and an upgrade from the original, which I believe was the intent.

Mouthfeel: Full in body but not the heavy undertaking that it appears to be. The carbonation goes nearly unnoticed, which is exactly what it's role should be in a beer this complex. Average drinkability for the style, although it's hard not to drink slowly savoring every sip.

Final Thoughts: My 500th review really snuck up on me and I'm glad I held onto this one all these months. It's residence in the fridge was well deserved. Definitely on par with the likes of BCBS, KBS, and the underrated Dragon's Milk but still in a category all its own. Would I drink it again? Of course, likely on tap at their destination brewery as soon as it's released this year.

750ml Bottle.

Starts with the Darkness base. The rye whiskey adds a subtle spiciness and some smooth barrel but no heavy char. Taste is hop-forward like the non-BA base at cooler temps, but as it warms the barrel steps forward and the flavor slides towards the nose, which is outstanding. The hops fade but do not disappear completely, and whiskey-soaked dark fruits emerge in their place.

Highlights are the smell and creamy (but not heavy) mouthfeel throughout, with the flavor rounding out nicely at warmer temps.

If you like the hop forward characteristics of the base Darkness, it's best to start drinking at a cooler temp and then let it warm to follow the transition. Great example of how the best BA stouts are those that start from strong base beers.

don1481, Jun 09, 2015
4.47/5  rDev -0.4%
look: 4.5 | smell: 4.25 | taste: 4.5 | feel: 4.75 | overall: 4.5

Poured from an artistically appointed 750 into snifters and goblets and enjoyed over 30 sinful minutes.

Pours an opaque chock-ebony that flops into the glass and evolves a khaki-tan cap of variegated bubbles. The leggy coating is as much glyercyl as it is sudsy, and although the head retains fairly well, it's the slick, clear oil that persists and creates a thin layer of sea-foam for the full depth of a glass.

Angular cherry suffocating under cocoa powder and roasted peaberry coffee beans is the first burst of the bouquet. The spice of cake and rum with dates make it a touch more sweet in the nose than I'd like, but the mix is compelling and draws me in for a taste. There's monocot and a breath of pine there, too, if you can restrain against the urge to gulp.

Early in the taste profile, it's chocolatey sweet, but the sucrose diminishes with time in the mouth to avoid a gangly imbalance. Drifting up in the middle is marshmallow, currant, and a chewy soft toffee that gives way to a foxy grip on the lateral tongue. As the sweetness fades, vanilla makes a brief appearance and then molasses ushers in the ending of smoky roast. The influence of the barrel age obscures any hoppy pine or squirt of citrus that might add to this complex experience...my only major knock on the taste.

Full bodied and truly milky in the mouth with a transition from heft to warming that lets a tiny sharp not of bitterness clean up the ending. I adore this part of of the drinking experience, wishing only for a small measure of extra bubbles to buzz through the middle so that it would enhance the flavor a touch more. But, the carbonation is just right for the finish, and the alcohol is cloaked beautifully without the need of anesthetic oils on the tongue.

Knowing that I'm new to the history of this legendary black beast, I apologize for a rating that may offend. But, I have no objection to the shadowy love and hellish layers of investment that have gone into refining this sinful indulgence. The brew represents one of the most important alcohol-based reasons to have moved to the Twin Cities...I can't wait to see how it ages in the bottle.

4.53/5  rDev +0.9%
look: 4.75 | smell: 4.75 | taste: 4.5 | feel: 4 | overall: 4.5

Serving: 750mL red-wax bottle poured into a Founder's CBS snifter. Bottled ca. October 2014, making this around 4 months old.

A: Pours a very viscous, pitch-black body. Nice swirling light tan head around the edges. Great lacing.

S: Lots of bourbon up front. Must have been aged quite a while in those barrels. Followed by layers of oak, char, vanilla, marshmallow. Hint of cinnamon as well. Just a touch of dark cherry and fig. Very reminiscent of Bell's Black Note.

T: Large bourbon presence in the taste here as well. Huge bourbon up front, followed by charred oak, wood, vanilla. Faint notes of chocolate near the back. Very oaky and boozy in the finish.

M: A bit thinner than expected. Decent carbonation. Booze isn't hidden very well, but that's not necessarily bad here. It's noticeable, but not yet overpowering.

O: I was pleasantly surprised by this beer. Never having had the original Darkness, it's unique to get my first exposure with the BA version. I can't compare to years previous, but this one is really nice. It's very traditional. I hardly picked up much chocolate and really no coffee notes. It's just a big, bad imperial stout with huge notes of bourbon from the aging process. This is probably the most bourbon forward and boozy BA Imp Stout I've had in a while. I normally don't like them this boozy, but there was something classic and bold about this one. I'll knock it for thin mouthfeel, and I also wish it was a bit more complex in flavor and dialed the heat back. But minor complaints in an otherwise wonderful beer.

Recommendation: Not sure if it's worth all the hassle some people go through to get this beer, but if you can spend a little time/effort chasing it, it's totally worth it (once!).

ramay86, May 31, 2015
3.94/5  rDev -12.2%
look: 3.75 | smell: 4 | taste: 4 | feel: 3.5 | overall: 4

A: The first distributed BA Darkness pours a dark brown, bordering on black, with a finger or so of beige/mocha head. The lacing is nothing to write home about, but it clearly displays alcohol legs. Thinner than past vintages of Darkness, but that is to be expected after barrel-aging. It is a solid-looking BA stout, but the lacing is very disappointing, as is the viscosity.

A: Whiskey leads off the aroma in a big way. Colder temperatures yield more "old-school" hops, more pine and grass than fruity citrus. Warmer temperatures allow heavy vanilla and chocolate to come through. Alcohol is evident. Oak tannins are present as well. The label says this is rye barrel-aged, but this is indistinguishable with bourbon. This is far boozier than it really should be, and far less balanced than non-BA Darknesses of the past.

T: The taste mostly follows the nose, being heavy on whiskey, vanilla, and moderate hoppiness. Chocolate and whiskey appear in the middle, as well as dark fruits. There are some cola flavors in the middle. Alcohol sweetness and malty sweetness along the lines of caramel and toffee appear and maintain from middle to end, becoming almost cloying towards the end. Compared to past versions, this is very sweet and relatively imbalanced. Disappointing, in a word. It is still a good-tasting beer, no question, but past versions of Darkness were superior due to the intense roast, hops, chocolate and toffee malts, and dark fruits all being in balance. The introduction of barrels only serves to make the beer sweeter and ruin the balance rather than adding depth as barrel-aging should.

M: Really very thin per style. Stickiness is great, but this is Central Waters year-rounds levels of mouthfeel. Alcohol is obvious in warming, and the moderately prevalent hop profile cannot cut the sweetness of the malt with any astringency or bitterness. No tannins to speak of either.

O: This is one of the few beers that I find did not become better on account of barrel-aging. It is good without question, but it comes off like Surly is behind the curve here. The barrel is far too heavy here, and rye is not evident at any point; without knowing, I would assume that this was barrel-aged, but in a million years I would not guess it was rye barrels. This is far thinner, far sweeter, and far less balanced than previous versions of Darkness. Perhaps Surly should consider not barrel-aging regular Darkness in the future and aging a unique beer as such, one tailored to the barrels. Barrel-aging an already great stout does not a great barrel-aged stout make; look to this and its antithesis, BCBS (Cook County Stout, a mediocre offering by all accounts) as evidence for this.
