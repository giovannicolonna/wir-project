Vintage 2012.

The colour is dark brown, almost black (90 EBC), the liquid is clear. It pours very smooth and silently. Almost no head, just a few brown bubbles. No lacing.

The smell is rich and smooth. Sweet notes of raisins and figs. Some alcohol, but at 15 % is is much less pronounced than expected. Flavours of mature whiskey, caramel and dark chocolate. Hints of tobacco and dark rum.

The taste is intensive. Lots of dark chocolate, dark rum and toffee. Coffee with sugar and some cream. Bourbon whiskey, liquor filled truffles and crème brûlée. Full body, if there ever was one beer with that. The aftertaste got passing very hot alcohol. Then, it gets sweeter with notes of dark rum, bourbon, molasses, dried figs and a touch of smoke. The finish is quite short with alcohol, dried fruits and coffee with whipped cream.

The carbonation is mild; the few small bubbles are fresh. The liquid is thick and sticky, almost like drinking syrup.

Yes, it is a very elegant and smooth beer with lots of deep flavours. But the hype fooled me, or is it just that barrel aged imperial stouts have got even heavier and more complex during the years? Still, a great dessert beer.

12 oz twist-off bottle poured into a snifter.
13% ABV, 2009, Bottled on: 11/12/09, "Serve in a snifter, Develops in the bottle for up to 5 years."

A - Odd this year was a twist off. An exceptionally hard twist-off at that. 2009 Bourbon County pours an inky black color which covers the snifter in long, gooey legs of alcohol but no lace is retained from the short-lived mocha-colored head.

S - Mellow warming alcohol in the form of bourbon arrives at the olfactory senses first, but sans the unnecessary burning solvent aromas the fresh bottle exhibits (though minor solvent opens up with a vigorous swirl). Rich chocolate comes in three parts following the bourbon; smooth milk chocolate, dark cocoa-rich chocolate, and bitter bakers chocolate on the finish which all intertwine nicely with the alcohol, vanilla and oak notes, and present a voluptuous backing.

T - Age lets the depths open up to further exploration in flavor, really showcasing the malt's qualities rather that make them fight to be experienced in this nearly 3-year-old bottle. Dark molasses and burnt caramel take precedence over the beer's vanilla and coffee bean flavors, but the malt really drives the aroma's chocolate flavors home. Soft, smooth bourbon and faint oak roll off on the tongue with near perfection, pairing well with, rather than oppressing, the beer.

M - When the beer clings to the glass altering it's color, it's a fair indicator that it will clutch your throat and gently glide down as you swallow, and that's exactly what this silky smooth, robust, full bodied beer does. Aged Bourbon County abandons the excessive heat and thinning solvent alcohol associated with fresh barrels without showing a collapse of the spirit's interaction. Instead, a supple warming finish creeps across the stomach with ninja-like subtlety.

O - I think it's quite fair to say that Goose Island's Bourbon County Stout truly becomes an exceptional beer with age, refining it's rougher "young" edges for a more sophisticated and approachable bold beer. The nose still remains somewhat intense concerning the alcohol, but the rest of the beer doesn't quite follow suit, opening new dimensions that are otherwise closed off due to the somewhat burdensome power of the spirit's freshness.

This three-year-old bottle indicates the beer is one gracefully cellaring beer; the reason I pick some up every year.

(On-tap serving selected to parallel old and new vintages)
